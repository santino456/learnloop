## [m1-foundations] Why Language World Models?

In the agent-environment loop, two complementary components are essential: the **policy** (states → actions) and the **world model** ((states, actions) → subsequent states). Current research on LLM agents has focused almost exclusively on the policy side. This module explains why world modeling is a necessary, not merely useful, missing piece on the path to general agents.

::: concept
title: World model

World model: a conditional text generator that predicts the next environment observation given the interaction history and the agent's current action. Formally, the LWM f_θ produces:

ô_{t+1} = f_θ(c, o_{≤t}, a_{≤t})

where c is the system prompt, o_t is the observation at turn t, and a_t is the agent's action. The training target is the ground-truth observation o_{t+1}.

Source: [Qwen-AgentWorld](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24597), §2.3
:::

::: concept
title: Policy-only agent training

Policy-only agent training: training that optimizes the state-to-action mapping without explicit next-state prediction. The agent learns to act but not to anticipate the consequences of its actions.

Source: [Qwen-AgentWorld](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24597), §1
:::

::: compare
left: Policy-only
right: World-model-augmented

- Aspect | Policy-only agent | World-model-augmented agent
- Core objective | Maximize task reward | Predict next state + maximize task reward
- Failure mode | Repeats errors until environment penalizes | Anticipates errors before acting
- Planning horizon | Single-step or heuristic | Multi-step via mental simulation
- Scalability | Bounded by real environment access | Scalable via simulated rollouts
- Training data | Agentic trajectories | Environment trajectories + agentic trajectories

Theoretical grounding: Richens et al. (2025) prove that any agent capable of generalizing across a sufficiently broad range of tasks must have learned a world model. Qwen-AgentWorld provides empirical support for this claim but does not independently prove necessity.

Source: [Qwen-AgentWorld](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24597), §1
:::

::: concept
title: Stateful vs. stateless environments

Stateful vs. stateless environments: In stateless environments (e.g., Search), state is carried implicitly in the conversation history. In stateful environments (e.g., Terminal, OS), an explicit internal state evolves with each action. In both cases the LWM operates over the same observation sequence.

Source: [Qwen-AgentWorld](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24597), §2.3
:::

## [m1-paradigms] Two Complementary Paradigms: Decouple vs. Unify

Qwen-AgentWorld explores two complementary strategies for applying world modeling to enhance language agents.

::: figure
src: assets/qwen-agentworld-fig-1.png
alt: Figure 1 overview of Qwen-AgentWorld showing the unified native language world model across seven domains and the two strategies Decouple and Unify.
caption: Figure 1: Overview of Qwen-AgentWorld. Top: Qwen-AgentWorld is a unified native language world model across seven domains. Bottom: We explore two complementary strategies for applying world modeling to enhance language agents (mainly using the 35B-A3B model as agent): Decouple and Unify, where the world model serves as the environment simulator and agent foundation model, respectively.
:::

::: concept
title: Decouple (simulator)

Decouple (simulator): The world model serves as a standalone environment simulator. The policy agent and the world model are separate models. This provides scalability (turn-level scaling of diverse environments without dedicated infrastructure) and controllability (targeted perturbations that expose agent weaknesses).

Source: [Qwen-AgentWorld](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24597), §6.1
:::

::: concept
title: Unify (foundation model)

Unify (foundation model): The same model that selects actions also predicts environment states. World-model training acts as a warm-up that improves downstream agent performance via prediction-driven action refinement. Next-state prediction is internalized as a meta-level thinking pattern similar to "reflection" but oriented toward the future.

Source: [Qwen-AgentWorld](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24597), §6.2
:::

::: decision
**When should you decouple vs. unify?**

--- perspective

**Choose Decouple when:**
- You need to scale environments beyond what real infrastructure permits (e.g., thousands of parallel training environments).
- You require controllable simulation for targeted perturbations or fictional-world construction.
- The simulator and agent have different optimal architectures or scales.

**Choose Unify when:**
- You want the agent to internalize mental simulation as a reasoning pattern.
- Infrastructure simplicity is preferred (one model instead of two).
- The same model scale suffices for both policy and world-model quality.

Source: [Qwen-AgentWorld](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24597), §1, §6
---
:::

## [m1-summary] Module Summary

- World models predict environment dynamics; they are theoretically necessary for general agents (Richens et al., 2025).
- Qwen-AgentWorld covers seven domains (MCP, Search, Terminal, SWE, Android, Web, OS) with a unified textual representation.
- The two paradigms—Decouple (simulator) and Unify (foundation model)—offer complementary paths to stronger agents.

::: exercise
**Perspective exercise**: You are designing a research agent that must read papers, run experiments, and write reports. Should you train or deploy it as a **Decouple** system (separate world model simulator) or a **Unify** system (single agent foundation model)?

--- perspective
Basis: The choice depends on whether the bottleneck is scalable simulation infrastructure or internalized reasoning.

- **Decouple** fits when you need thousands of cheap simulated paper-review or experiment-design rollouts to stress-test the agent, or when the simulator can be shared across multiple agent variants.
- **Unify** fits when the agent must carry world-model-like anticipation inside its own reasoning trace during real deployment, and when maintaining two large models is impractical.

There is no universal winner: the two paradigms are complementary, and a mature pipeline may use Decouple during training and Unify at inference.

:::
